College Student Interview

Quick Summary

I talked to a student who is studying computational biology at Brown University.

I asked him about his stance and experiences with the ethical concerns and implications of genetic engineering and how it should or should not be used in society.

Interview

I talked to Brian Kirz, a college student who is studying computational biology at Brown University. I asked him about what he thinks about how we should or should not go about using genetic engineering in society and how he belives it should be distributed and allowed in the future.

He states that he belives that it should be available and distributed to everyone fairly, meaning that everyone should have access to it, by it either being paid for by the government or by the individual. He additionally said that he belives it's revolutionary and would make way groundbreaking scientific advances in the future.

I also asked him about what he thinks of genetic screening of sperm and eggs to control and prevent any unwanted genetic mutations, and how he views the way it should, or not, be done.

Brain said that he belives that it should be allowed only if it is to prevent serious, life-damaging diseases, and not to prevent small, or irrelevant conditions / diseases or just to select the "fastest, better, stronger" embryos.

I then followed up with him about why he views this differently from how he views other genetic engineering methods such as actually altering the DNA of live, pre- existing cells of organisms. To which he said that he actually views them both the same, because he meant that he only belives that the actual modification of live cells and DNA of living organisms should only be allowed if it is to fix those previously said life-altering and life-damaging diseases, and not to just make the fully grown adult "better, faster, stronger", etc.

Aditionally, just out of curiosity, I also asked him about what he thinks about vaccines and their availability and even potential imbalance they might cause. This is because in some places, such as Brazil for example, only vaccines that are absolutely crucial and necessary are actually given out for free. On the other hand, vaccines that aren't for diseases as serious, are not given out for free, thus being a direct contradiction to being able to the argument of not being able to screen gametes before fertilization, as it allows the rich, who have access to this technology to be genetically superior to the poor, who don't; and although both are not the same, it is a similar argument / discussion.

To this, he responded that he thinks that vaccines should be available for free for everyone, as is in lots of developed countries or even just a lot more affordable. He also rebutted that in most countries already, vaccines are already available for free, to which I agree with.

Reflection

Overall, I belive I benefitted immensely from this interview. I got tons of insights into the ethical concerns and implications that are present in an actual academic enviroment and how they should or should not be applied and utilized. I also got to realize that the way I was thinking about genetic engineering in the past, was not necessarily the way I would be thinking about it now, as it has allowed me to see the whole field from another perpespective. Aditionally, although I didn't completely agree with all of his ideas and arguments, I understood where he was coming from, and we had a lot of really interesting and thought provoking conversations. In conclusion, it was great to talk to Brian about this, and it was a super interesting conversation and experience.
